Which SaaS Tools Are Actually Worth It for Small Businesses, and Which Are Not

📅 December 2024 ⏱ 6 min read

There are currently around 30,000 SaaS products on the market. That is not an exaggeration, it is reality, and the market keeps growing. For small businesses, that means the pressure to use the "right" tool is enormous, while the budget is limited and time is even tighter.

Over the last few years, I have tried a lot of tools, both for myself and for clients. Here is my honest take, with no affiliate links and no favors involved.

The honest truth upfront

Most small businesses need fewer tools, not more. The biggest problem is not having the wrong tool. It is having too many tools, each used a little, while none of them truly supports the business properly. Fewer tools, used consistently, always beat a bigger stack used half heartedly.

The rule of thumb is simple: a tool is worth it if it saves time, prevents mistakes or enables revenue you otherwise would not have. Everything else is nice to have.

Category 1: Communication and project management

This is where it is decided whether a small team stays coordinated or sinks into chaos. The good news is that the best tools in this category are free or very affordable.

Free
Notion

Wiki, project plan, client database and notes, all in one place. There is a learning curve, but it is worth it.

Free
Trello

A simple Kanban board. Perfect for straightforward task tracking without unnecessary overhead.

From €7 per month
Slack

For teams of three people or more, it is clearly better than WhatsApp groups. Channels keep topics properly separated.

Free
Google Workspace Lite

Docs, Sheets and Drive are free. For many small businesses, that is completely enough.

Category 2: Customer acquisition and CRM

A lot of businesses invest too early in tools that are too expensive. A CRM only starts making sense once you actually have more leads than you can realistically keep track of in your head.

When you do not need a CRM yet

If you are managing fewer than 20 to 30 active contacts at the same time, a well maintained spreadsheet is often enough. Seriously. HubSpot, Pipedrive and similar tools are powerful, but they need upkeep and onboarding. If you do not use them consistently, you end up paying every month for an empty database.

When you should start

When you notice that you forget leads, send follow up emails too late or no longer know where a contact stands in the process, that is the moment to start. HubSpot CRM has a solid free tier, and that is fully enough in the beginning.

Category 3: Accounting and administration

This is the category that saves the most time and is neglected most often. Anyone still writing invoices manually in Word is losing hours every single week.

From €9 per month
Lexoffice

Invoices, quotes and DATEV export. It is the standard choice for German solo entrepreneurs and SMEs.

From €10 per month
Sevdesk

An alternative to Lexoffice with a slightly more modern interface. Both do a good job.

What you can safely ignore

A lot of things in the SaaS market are marketed well, but for small businesses they are simply too large, too complex or too expensive. My short list of overrated tools for small businesses is:

  • Salesforce, built for enterprises, a nightmare for solo operators
  • Jira, brilliant for development teams, overkill for almost everything else
  • Marketing automation suites, if you do not have a stable flow of leads, you are just wasting setup time
  • Any tool with 50 plus features that you did not fully look at during onboarding

Conclusion: keep your stack small and use it consistently

My recommendation for a healthy tool stack in a small business is this: no more than 5 to 6 tools that you use daily and fully understand. It is better to replace a tool that does not fit than to stack a new one on top of it.

And if you are thinking about building your own SaaS tool, there is a right timing for that too, and a wrong order to do it in. I will write more about that soon.
